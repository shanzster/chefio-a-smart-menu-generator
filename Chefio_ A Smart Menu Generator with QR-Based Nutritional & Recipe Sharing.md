# **INTRODUCTION**

The global hospitality and culinary landscape is undergoing a profound digital transformation, often referred to in academic literature as *"Culinary Informatics"* or *"Gastronomy 4.0."* In the 21st century, the preparation of food has transcended mere artistry and manual skill. It now intersects heavily with data analytics, nutritional science, and digital user experience. According to recent industry reports, the integration of technology in food systems. From farm-to-table tracking to AI-driven recipe development has become a standard expectation rather than a novelty. This shift is driven by a heightened global health consciousness, where consumers demand transparency regarding caloric intake, macronutrients, and ingredient sourcing (Mintel, 2023). Consequently, the role of the modern chef and the hospitality manager has evolved. It is no longer sufficient to merely execute a recipe. Professionals are now expected to be managers of information, ensuring that dietary restrictions are met with precision and that nutritional data is communicated effectively to the consumer.

This technological wave has inevitably reached the shores of education, specifically within Hotel and Restaurant Management (HRM) and culinary arts programs. The rising trend in vocational education is moving towards smart learning environments where theoretical knowledge is augmented by digital tools. Progressive Web Applications (PWAs) and mobile platforms are increasingly used to simulate kitchen management scenarios, inventory control, and menu engineering. Furthermore, the post-pandemic dining era has normalized the use of QR codes as a primary interface for customer interaction, transitioning menus from static physical artifacts to dynamic digital portals. This normalization presents a unique opportunity for educational institutions to adopt similar technologies, preparing students for a workforce that relies on digital agility as much as culinary proficiency.

Despite the rapid advancement of food technologies, significant  problems persist within the academic framework of HRM and culinary training. The primary challenge lies in the cognitive load associated with menu planning and nutritional analysis. Students often struggle to synthesize constraints such as available ingredients, specific dietary requirements, and strict preparation times into a cohesive menu. Traditional methods of teaching menu engineering often involve manual calculations and static reference books, which can be prone to error and time-consuming, detaching the student from the creative process of cooking.

Moreover, a specific pedagogical gap exists in the presentation and evaluation phase of culinary education. In typical setups like cook-offs, practical classes, or capstone events, the interaction between the student-chef and the judge or guest is often fleeting. Dishes are presented physically, but the depth of information of the dish, its precise nutritional breakdown, and the recipe logic is frequently lost or relegated to printed handouts that are easily discarded. There is a lack of real-time information transfer; a judge tasting a dish cannot instantly view the macronutrient profile or the preparation steps without disrupting the flow of service.

Furthermore, while various applications exist in the market, they rarely cater to the specific needs of the student learner. Commercial apps like DishGen or ChefGPT focus heavily on the end-user or home cook, utilizing AI for simple recipe generation without the educational scaffolding required for HRM students. These tools often lack the smart integration of inventory-based logic and professional-grade nutritional analysis necessary for academic rigor. Conversely, enterprise-level food management systems used in hotels are prohibitively expensive and too complex for classroom environments.

To address these gaps, the proponent proposes the development of **Chefio: A Smart Menu Generator with QR-Based Nutritional & Recipe Sharing.** The primary function of Chefio is the fusion of Information Technology and Hospitality Management principles into a cohesive educational tool. Born from an interdisciplinary approach often leveraging IT background within an HRM context. **Chefio** is designed to serve as a digital sous-chef for students. Unlike generic recipe apps, Chefio is grounded in the logic of **availability and optimization.** It allows users to input existing inventory, dietary preferences, and time constraints to generate scientifically viable menu options, thereby teaching students the critical skill of resource management.

## **1.1 Background of the Study**

The food and hospitality industry is changing rapidly due to modern technology. Restaurants and hotels now use computer systems to manage their inventory and plan their menus. This shift helps professionals save time and reduce waste. At the same time, people are becoming more conscious about their health. They want to know exactly what is in their food. They look for information about calories and nutrients before they eat. Because of these trends, culinary schools need to teach students how to use technology alongside their cooking skills.

In the academic setting, Hotel and Restaurant Management (HRM) students face many challenges. They must learn how to cook delicious meals while also managing costs and nutritional content. Menu planning is a difficult task that requires balancing available ingredients with dietary rules. Students often do this process manually. They spend hours calculating portion sizes and nutritional values by hand. This manual process is slow and prone to mistakes. It takes time away from the actual cooking and creative process.

The concept for this project, **Chefio**, originated from direct observation within the classroom. The proponent has a background in Information Technology and is currently completing subjects in Hotel and Restaurant Management. This unique position provided a clear view of the difficulties students face. It was observed that many students struggle to create menus when ingredients are limited. They also find it hard to present their dishes professionally during practical exams.

During cook-offs and practical classes, there is a disconnect between the chef and the audience. Judges and guests taste the food, but they often miss the details behind the dish. Printed recipe cards are frequently ignored or misplaced. There is no easy way for a guest to see the nutritional breakdown or the preparation steps in real time. This lack of information reduces the educational value of the event.

Existing mobile applications do not fully solve these problems. Most recipe apps are designed for home cooks rather than students. They suggest random recipes but do not help with strict portion control or academic requirements. They also lack features for sharing information instantly in a classroom setting.

Chefio was designed to fill this gap. It combines the logic of Information Technology with the creativity of Culinary Arts. The system uses an algorithm to generate menus based on what is available. It automates the calculation of nutrition facts. Most importantly, it introduces QR code technology to student presentations. This allows judges to scan a code and instantly view the recipe details on their phones. This project aims to use technology to make culinary training more efficient, interactive, and informative.

**1.2 Statement of the Problem**

### ***1.2.1 General Problem***

The primary problem is the significant time inefficiency and logistical burden placed on HRM students during their daily culinary activities. Students currently spend a disproportionate amount of time on manual menu planning, nutritional calculations, and recipe management, which subtracts valuable hours from practical cooking and skill refinement. This lack of a streamlined, automated system creates a bottleneck in their day-to-day workflow, limiting their ability to maximize learning outcomes and efficiently manage their culinary tasks.

### ***1.2.2 Specific Problems***

1. HRM students rely on manual research and trial-and-error to plan menus based on limited ingredients, which leads to excessive time consumption and reduces the time available for actual food preparation.  
2. Culinary learners perform nutritional analysis and portion control calculations by hand, which leads to a slow, error-prone process that hampers their daily productivity and focus on culinary techniques.  
3. The current method of presenting dishes involves static, printed materials or repetitive verbal explanations, which leads to inefficient information transfer and consumes valuable time that could be used for meaningful feedback and improvement.  
4. Students lack an instant, data-driven reference for ingredient substitutions, which leads to operational delays during cooking sessions when specific items are unavailable.  
5. The absence of a centralized tool for managing daily recipes and nutritional data forces students to use disjointed methods (such as physical notes or disparate apps), which leads to a disorganized workflow and prevents them from seamlessly enhancing their day-to-day culinary portfolio.

## **1.3 Objectives of the Study**

### ***1.3.1 General Objectives***

The general objective of this study is to develop **"Chefio," a Smart Menu Generator** that streamlines the daily culinary workflow of HRM students. The system aims to significantly reduce the time spent on manual menu planning and nutritional analysis by automating these processes, thereby allowing students to focus more on practical skill development and day-to-day kitchen productivity.

### ***1.3.2 Specific Objectives***

To achieve the general objective, the proponent aims to accomplish the following:

1. To develop an automated menu generation module that instantly suggests dishes based on available inventory and constraints, eliminating the excessive time consumed by manual trial-and-error planning.  
2. To integrate a real-time nutritional analysis engine that automates the calculation of macronutrients and portion sizes, preventing manual computation errors and speeding up the students' daily preparation process.  
3. To implement a QR code sharing feature that replaces static printed materials with digital links, facilitating the efficient and immediate transfer of recipe and nutritional information to judges or guests.  
4. To incorporate an intelligent ingredient substitution tool that provides instant alternatives for missing items, preventing operational delays and maintaining momentum during cooking sessions.  
5. To provide a centralized, digital platform for organizing recipes and culinary data, resolving the issue of disjointed workflows and enabling students to manage their day-to-day culinary portfolio effectively.

## **1.5 The Scope and Limitations**

### ***1.5.1 The Scope***

This study focuses on the development of "Chefio," a Progressive Web Application (PWA) designed to streamline the menu planning and presentation workflow for HRM students. The system covers the automation of menu generation, nutritional calculation, and digital information sharing via QR codes.

1. **User Levels**

The capabilities of the system are organized by the user levels defined below:

* **Cooks.** This user level is responsible for inputting ingredients, generating menus, managing their digital recipe portfolio, and utilizing the nutritional analysis tools for their dishes.  
  * **Guest.** This user level is responsible for scanning QR codes to access recipe details, viewing nutritional information, and providing feedback or ratings during culinary evaluations.  
  * **Admin.** This user level is responsible for managing user account information and the database configuration.  
2. **Modules**  
   * ***Cooks Accounts***  
     * **Authentication Module.** This module allows this user to log in to the system.  
     * **Meal Generation Module.** This module enables the student to input available ingredients, dietary constraints, and preparation time. The system then uses several artificial intelligent (AI) models to generate viable recipe suggestions.  
     * **Nutritional Analysis Module.** Using AI models, the module automatically calculates the total calories and macronutrients (protein, fats, carbohydrates) for selected recipes. This replaces manual computation of the nutritional value of a meal.  
     * **Recipe Recommendation Module.** This module allows the user to find a specific meal’s recipe and check their ingredients. In the case that the user doesn’t have the necessary ingredients, the AI model recommends an alternative recipe to create it. They will also provide a note saying that the dish can be done without this ingredient.   
     * **QR Code Generation Module.** This module converts the final recipe details as well as the nutritional values into a unique QR code that can be downloaded or printed for display.  
   * ***Guest Accounts***  
     * **Public Viewing Interface.** A simplified, mobile-friendly page accessed by scanning a QR code. It displays the dish name, ingredients, nutritional facts, and preparation steps. No login is required for this view.  
     * **Feedback Module.** Allows the guest or judge to provide a simple rating or comment regarding the dish they scanned.  
   * ***Admin Accounts***  
     * **User Management Module.** Allows the admin to view, suspend, or delete user accounts if they violate system policies.  
     * **Dashboard and Analytics.** Provides a view of how many recipes are being generated and how many users are active on the platform.  
     * **AI Model Configuration Module.** This module allows administrators to define how the AI responds, what content it can generate, and which features are enabled or restricted. Admins can configure safety parameters, adjust creativity levels, and apply filters based on the system’s requirements.

### ***1.5.2 The Limitations***

1. As a Progressive Web Application, the system requires an active internet connection to generate menus, perform AI feasibility checks, and fetch nutritional data. It cannot function fully offline.  
2. The accuracy of the nutritional analysis depends on the third-party food database API used by the system. If an ingredient is not in the database, the system may not provide an accurate calculation.  
3. Guests and judges must have a smartphone with a working camera and a QR code scanning feature to view the recipe details.  
4. The AI's determination of whether a dish can be cooked without an ingredient is based on general culinary rules. It may not account for specific textural or flavor requirements of highly specialized or traditional regional dishes.
